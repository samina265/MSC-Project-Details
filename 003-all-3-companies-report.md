# Initial Investigation: Comparison of Corporate Sustainability Reports for Scope 3 Reporting

## Objective

To investigate how leading multinational companies disclose Scope 3 greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and identify common reporting challenges before defining the research direction.

**Companies Reviewed**

1. Microsoft – 2025 Environmental Sustainability Report
2. Apple – 2025 Environmental Progress Report
3. Google – 2025 Environmental Report

---

# 1. Common Findings

Although Microsoft, Apple, and Google have different reporting styles, several important similarities were identified.

## 1.1 All companies disclose Scope 1, Scope 2 and Total Scope 3 emissions

Each company reports:

* Scope 1 emissions
* Scope 2 emissions
* Total Scope 3 emissions
* Climate targets
* Net-zero commitments

This indicates that large multinational companies generally follow international sustainability reporting standards.

---

## 1.2 All companies identify the supply chain as the largest source of emissions

Across all three reports, supply chain activities contribute the majority of total emissions.

Examples include:

* Purchased goods and services
* Manufacturing
* Capital goods
* Supplier operations
* Transportation
* Product life cycle

Therefore, Scope 3—not Scope 1 or Scope 2—is the dominant emissions source.

---

## 1.3 Supplier data remain the biggest reporting challenge

A common observation across the reports is that collecting reliable supplier emissions data is difficult.

Google explicitly states that:

> "Gathering accurate data on indirect emissions from supply chains remains a challenge."

The report also notes:

> "Data gaps and evolving standards" remain major challenges for indirect emissions reporting.

These statements align closely with findings from recent academic literature, which reports that companies struggle to calculate Scope 3 because supplier activity data are often unavailable, incomplete, or inconsistent.

---

## 1.4 Companies rely on supplier engagement

Rather than directly measuring supplier emissions themselves, all three companies describe programs to improve supplier reporting.

Examples include:

* Supplier renewable energy initiatives
* Supplier engagement programmes
* Supplier carbon reduction targets
* Supplier emissions disclosure

This suggests that obtaining complete primary supplier data remains an ongoing challenge.

---

# 2. Differences Between the Reports

Although all three companies report Scope 3 emissions, the level of detail varies considerably.

---

## Microsoft

### Strengths

* Reports Scope 3 according to the GHG Protocol categories.
* Provides detailed category-level emissions.
* Clearly separates emissions such as:

  * Purchased Goods & Services
  * Capital Goods
  * Business Travel
  * Employee Commuting
  * Waste
  * Downstream Transportation
  * Use of Sold Products

### Limitation

Microsoft reports emissions by category but does not disclose the underlying activity data (e.g., kilograms of materials purchased or transportation distances).

---

## Apple

### Strengths

Apple focuses heavily on product life-cycle emissions.

Major topics include:

* Manufacturing
* Transportation
* Product use
* Recycling
* Supplier clean energy
* Recycled materials

Apple also provides detailed information on recycled material content and supplier decarbonization programmes.

### Limitation

Apple does not consistently report emissions using all individual GHG Protocol Scope 3 categories.

Several categories are aggregated under broader life-cycle stages.

---

## Google

### Strengths

Google focuses on operational decarbonization and AI-enabled sustainability.

Major topics include:

* Data centres
* Artificial Intelligence
* Renewable electricity
* Clean energy procurement
* Supplier renewable energy

The report openly discusses the practical challenges of Scope 3 reporting.

### Limitation

Compared with Microsoft, Google provides less detailed reporting for individual Scope 3 categories.

Instead, the report focuses more on organisational strategy and operational improvements.

# 4. Key Observation

The investigation revealed that the main challenge is **not the absence of sustainability reports**.

Instead, the primary challenge is the **inconsistency and incompleteness of Scope 3 reporting**.

Examples include:

* Different reporting structures across companies.
* Different levels of Scope 3 category detail.
* Missing activity data required for GHG calculations.
* Heavy reliance on supplier-reported information.
* Limited availability of primary supplier emissions data.

---

# 5. Preliminary Research Gap

Current sustainability reports successfully disclose total Scope 3 emissions, but they frequently lack complete and consistent supplier-related activity data.

This creates several practical challenges:

* Missing Scope 3 categories.
* Incomplete supplier information.
* Difficulties in calculating emissions using the GHG Protocol.
* Limited comparability between companies.

These observations suggest that artificial intelligence and machine learning may be useful for assisting—not replacing—the existing GHG Protocol methodology by estimating missing information where direct supplier data are unavailable.

---

# Conclusion

The comparison indicates that although Microsoft, Apple, and Google all report Scope 3 emissions, they differ substantially in reporting detail and methodology. A common issue across the reports is the difficulty of obtaining complete supplier-related data for indirect emissions.

This consistent reporting challenge provides a strong motivation for exploring AI-assisted methods to support more complete and consistent Scope 3 reporting in future research.
