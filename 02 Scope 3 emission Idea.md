Let me read pages 7–10 so I summarize the actual 15 categories from this document.Here are the 15 categories, explained like you're 10.
First, the big idea: Scope 3 is all the pollution a company causes that doesn't come from its own chimneys or its own electricity bill — it's the pollution that happens because of the company, but at other people's places. Like how a birthday party makes a mess at the bakery, in the delivery van, and in your trash can later — not just in your kitchen.
There are two halves. Upstream = pollution from stuff happening before it reaches the company (making and buying things). Downstream = pollution from stuff happening after the company sells things (using and throwing them away).
Upstream (1–8): the "buying" side
Purchased goods and services — all the stuff the company buys to make its products, like ingredients and materials.
Capital goods — the big, long-lasting things it buys, like machines, buildings, and trucks.
Fuel and energy stuff — the pollution from making and delivering the fuel and electricity, before the company even burns or uses it.
Upstream transport — trucks, ships, and planes bringing the company its supplies (in vehicles it doesn't own).
Waste from operations — the garbage the company makes, and the pollution from someone else dealing with it.
Business travel — employees flying and driving for work trips.
Employee commuting — workers traveling from home to the office and back.
Upstream leased assets — things the company rents and uses, like a rented warehouse.
Downstream (9–15): the "selling" side
Downstream transport — delivering the finished products to shops and customers.
Processing of sold products — when another company takes the product and does more work on it (like a factory turning your sold steel into a car).
Use of sold products — pollution from customers using what they bought (a sold car burning gas, a sold fridge using power). This one is often huge.
End-of-life treatment — pollution from throwing the product away once the customer is done with it.
Downstream leased assets — things the company owns and rents out to others.
Franchises — pollution from franchise shops (like one restaurant brand's many locations).
Investments — pollution from companies this company has invested money in.
The simplest way to remember the split: categories 1–8 are about what the company brings in, and 9–15 are about what it sends out into the world.
